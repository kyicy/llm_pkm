## Course Overview

MIT 18.01 Single Variable Calculus is a complete first course in calculus covering differentiation, integration, series, and their applications. The course builds from the definition of the derivative through techniques of integration to Taylor series and improper integrals.

- **Format:** 38 lectures + 4 exams, Fall 2006 semester
- **Instructor:** Prof. David Jerison
- **Prerequisites:** High school precalculus (algebra, trigonometry, functions)
- **Textbook:** Simmons, *Calculus with Analytic Geometry* (optional)

**Key skills:** Compute derivatives from the limit definition; apply product, quotient, and chain rules; differentiate trigonometric, exponential, log, and hyperbolic functions; use implicit differentiation; prove $\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}$ for all real $n$.

**Highlight — Geometric insight:** The derivative $f'(x_0)$ is the slope of the tangent line. For $f(x) = 1/x$ at $x_0$, the tangent line always forms a triangle with area 2, regardless of where on the curve the tangent is drawn.

**Highlight — Counterintuitive result:** For a constrained optimization (e.g., cutting wire into two squares), the minimum area is achieved at an interior critical point, but the **maximum** area is achieved at the endpoints — never forget to check endpoints.

**Key skills:** Set up and evaluate Riemann sums; apply FTC 1 and 2; compute areas between curves; find volumes using disks/washers and cylindrical shells; compute average values; use Simpson's rule and trapezoidal rule; evaluate the Gaussian integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}$.

**Highlight — Two different volumes, same equation:** The witch's cauldron $y=x^2$ rotated about the y-axis has volume $\pi a^2/2$. But the equation is not scale-invariant — the same formula in different units produces physically different shapes. Always track units.

**Key skills:** Evaluate trigonometric integrals using identities and substitution; integrate rational functions by partial fractions; apply integration by parts; compute arc length and surface area; work in polar coordinates; use L'Hôpital's rule for indeterminate forms; determine convergence of improper integrals and infinite series; compute Taylor series expansions.

**Highlight — The stacking problem:** The harmonic series $\sum 1/n$ diverges. This means you can stack identical blocks to extend arbitrarily far horizontally. To span a typical lecture hall (~30 ft), the stack would need to be ~$10^{26}$ meters high — about the diameter of the observable universe.
