## Overview

The Feynman Technique is a four-step learning method developed by Nobel laureate physicist Richard Feynman. Its core premise is that true understanding is demonstrated by the ability to explain a concept simply—jargon and complexity often mask gaps in comprehension. The technique is universally applicable across subjects and emphasizes active recall, simplification, and iterative refinement.

## The Four Steps

### 1. Select and Map

Choose a concept and write down everything you already know about it on a blank page. Use a different color to add new information as you learn, creating a visual map of growing understanding.

### 2. Explain to a Child

Write down the idea using language that a 12-year-old would understand. This step forces you to strip away technical jargon and identify the core essence of the concept. If you cannot explain it simply, you have not yet grasped it fully.

Writing serves multiple purposes: it encourages clearer thinking, organizes thoughts, reveals gaps in understanding, and creates material for future review.

### 3. Review and Refine

Re-reading your explanation exposes weak spots and areas where understanding is shallow. When you find gaps, return to the source material and study until you can explain the section simply. Rewrite improved sections as your understanding deepens.

### 4. Test and Archive

Test your understanding by teaching someone else without referring to your notes. Once satisfied, archive the simple explanation in a learning binder for periodic review. Regular review compounds understanding and helps develop new ideas.

## Core Insight

The technique is grounded in the principle that one must understand the true essence of a concept—not merely know its name. Feynman's approach treats learning as an active process of distillation and teaching, rather than passive consumption of information.
